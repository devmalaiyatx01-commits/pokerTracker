How the opponents "think"

Each bot makes decisions with a tiny neural network: a hand-strength estimate, pot odds, position, stack depth, and how many opponents remain all feed in as numbers, get combined through a weighted sum, and come out as three probabilities — fold, call, or raise. That's a real forward pass through a network, the same basic computation behind any deep learning model.

What makes the five bots play differently isn't training — it's that each one starts with different hand-tuned weights, the same way a "tight" poker player and a "loose" poker player are both using judgment, just calibrated differently. Preflop hand strength is estimated with the classic Chen formula; postflop it's estimated by simulating dozens of random possible opponent hands and remaining boards (a small Monte Carlo simulation) and checking how often your bot's hand would win.

The tracker panel on the right is doing what real poker-tracking software (like PokerTracker or Hold'em Manager) does: counting how often each opponent voluntarily enters a pot (VPIP), how often they raise first in (PFR), and their ratio of bets/raises to calls (aggression factor) — then turning those numbers into a plain-English read on their style.
